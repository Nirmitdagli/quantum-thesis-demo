In Simple Words

Cybersecurity is getting harder every day. This thesis explores whether quantum computers — a fundamentally new type of computer — can help fight cyber attacks better than traditional machines. We built a complete system that runs three quantum experiments, each targeting a real cybersecurity problem, and compared them on accuracy, speed, and energy consumption across five cloud platforms.

Think of it like this...

Imagine you're a librarian protecting a building. A regular computer is like having a single security guard checking IDs one at a time. A quantum computer is like having a guard who can check everyone's IDs at the same time — because in the quantum world, you can look at many possibilities simultaneously. Our thesis asked: Is this quantum guard actually better at catching intruders? Is it faster? Does it use less electricity? We tested all three questions and found the answers.

Glossary

Every Term Explained

All the quantum computing and algorithm terms used in this thesis, in plain English

Quantum Computing Terms

Qubit (Quantum Bit)
A normal computer bit is either 0 or 1, like a light switch — ON or OFF. A qubit can be 0, 1, or both at the same time. Imagine a coin spinning in the air — it's not heads or tails, it's BOTH until you look. That's a qubit. With just 4 spinning coins (qubits), you represent 16 combinations at once!
|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ where |α|² + |β|² = 1
Superposition
The ability of a qubit to be in multiple states at once. Think of a coin spinning in the air — it's both heads and tails until it lands. With 4 qubits in superposition, you can represent all 16 possible combinations simultaneously.
Entanglement
Two qubits linked so measuring one instantly determines the other, no matter the distance. Imagine two magic dice — you roll one in New York and one in Tokyo. They ALWAYS show the same number. Einstein called it "spooky action at a distance." This lets quantum computers coordinate calculations in ways classical machines simply cannot.
QPU (Quantum Processing Unit)
The quantum equivalent of a CPU or GPU. It's the physical chip that contains and manipulates qubits. Companies like IBM, Google, and IonQ build QPUs using superconducting circuits or trapped ions cooled near absolute zero.
Quantum Gate
An operation that changes a qubit's state, like logic gates (AND, OR, NOT) in normal computers. Common gates: H (creates superposition), CNOT (entangles two qubits), RZ (rotates phase). A quantum algorithm is a sequence of gates.
Gate Fidelity
How accurately a quantum gate performs. 99.7% fidelity means it works correctly 997 out of 1000 times. Higher fidelity = better results. This is why platform choice matters — Quantinuum has 99.7% vs IBM's 99.0%.
Shots
How many times you run a quantum circuit and measure. Since quantum results are probabilistic, you run the same circuit many times (we used 2,000 shots) and look at the statistics of outcomes.
Statevector
The complete mathematical description of a quantum system's state. For 4 qubits, it's a list of 16 complex numbers. Statevector simulation gives exact results (no randomness) but only works for small numbers of qubits.
Oracle
The "cheese detector" in our mouse maze. It doesn't tell the mouse WHERE the cheese is — it just says "you're standing on it!" or "nope, keep looking." The oracle marks the right answer with a special quantum flag (phase flip). Grover's algorithm then amplifies that flag so it becomes obvious.
Diffusion Operator
After the cheese detector (oracle) marks the room, the diffusion operator is like a magnifying glass for probability. It makes the cheese room "louder" and everything else "quieter." After 3 rounds of mark-and-amplify, the cheese room is screaming "HERE I AM!" at 96.4% volume.
Hamiltonian
Think of it as a landscape of hills and valleys. Each valley is a possible solution; the deepest valley is the best answer. The Hamiltonian is the map of this landscape. QAOA tries to roll a quantum ball downhill to find the deepest valley — but it can quantum-tunnel through hills to avoid getting stuck!
Feature Map / ZZ Feature Map
A quantum circuit that encodes classical data into quantum states. The ZZ Feature Map uses Hadamard + phase gates for individual features, then CNOT pairs for feature interactions. This maps data into a high-dimensional quantum space where patterns become easier to separate.

Algorithm & ML Terms

SVM (Support Vector Machine)
Imagine you have a bunch of red dots (attacks) and blue dots (normal traffic) on a table. SVM draws the widest possible line between them so new dots can be classified. It's like building a wall between two neighborhoods — the thicker the wall, the better you can tell which side a new house belongs to.
Kernel (in Machine Learning)
A "similarity detector." Given two data points, how alike are they? Like comparing two faces: a classical kernel (RBF) compares eye color, nose size, hair. A quantum kernel compares those PLUS millions of invisible micro-features that humans can't see — it operates in exponentially more dimensions.
MaxCut
Imagine a spider web. You need to cut it into two pieces with one slice that breaks the most threads. With 7 threads, you can try all options. With 50? There are more possibilities than atoms in the universe. This is NP-hard — even the fastest supercomputer can't solve it at large scale. But quantum computers can get close.
QSVM (Quantum Support Vector Machine)
Regular SVM + quantum superpowers. Instead of comparing data with a classical "similarity detector," QSVM encodes each data point into a quantum state (using 4 qubits for our 4 features) and measures overlap. It's like upgrading your security camera from HD to a billion-pixel quantum sensor that sees invisible patterns in network traffic.
QAOA (Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm)
Like a DJ mixing two tracks to find the perfect beat. Track 1 (cost layer) rewards good solutions, Track 2 (mixer layer) explores new ideas. The DJ (optimizer) adjusts the volume of each track over many tries until the music (solution) sounds perfect. The quantum part? The DJ can hear ALL possible mixes at once.
Grover's Algorithm
Like a mouse with super-smell finding cheese in a maze. A classical mouse checks every room one by one (1,000,000 rooms = 1,000,000 checks). Grover's quantum mouse sends out a "wave" that bounces back stronger from the cheese room — finds it in just ~1,000 sniffs. That's the quadratic speedup: √N instead of N.
COBYLA (Optimizer)
A classical optimization algorithm used to tune quantum circuit parameters. It adjusts gamma and beta values in QAOA by trying different combinations and gradually finding the best settings. Doesn't need gradients — ideal for noisy quantum results.
Approximation Ratio
Like a grade on a test. If the perfect answer is 100%, our QAOA scored 88.3% while the classical greedy approach scored 85.7%. In the real world, for NP-hard problems (ones that are basically impossible to solve perfectly), scoring above 80% is like getting an A. QAOA's score improves with more qubits.

Metrics & Evaluation Terms

Accuracy
The percentage of correct predictions. If a model correctly identifies 95 out of 100 network packets as normal or attack, that's 95% accuracy. Simple but can be misleading if data is unbalanced.
Accuracy = (TP + TN) / (TP + TN + FP + FN)
Precision
Of all the items the model flagged as attacks, what percentage were actually attacks? High precision = few false alarms. If it flagged 10 packets as malicious and 9 really were, precision is 90%.
Precision = TP / (TP + FP)
Recall
Of all actual attacks, what percentage did the model catch? High recall = few missed attacks. If there were 10 real attacks and the model caught 8, recall is 80%. In security, missing attacks is dangerous, so recall matters a lot.
Recall = TP / (TP + FN)
F1 Score
The "overall grade" that balances precision and recall. Like a student's GPA combining all subjects. F1 = 1.0 means perfect security: every alarm is real AND every real attack is caught. F1 = 0.5 means your system is mediocre — missing attacks or crying wolf too often. Our QSVM achieved F1 = 1.0 (perfect!).
F1 = 2 × (Precision × Recall) / (Precision + Recall)
Confusion Matrix
A report card for security. Four boxes: "Caught the bad guys" (True Positive), "Let good guys through" (True Negative), "Falsely accused an innocent person" (False Positive), "Let a bad guy escape" (False Negative). The last two are the mistakes — our quantum model had ZERO of both!
PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness)
A data center efficiency metric. PUE = Total Facility Energy / IT Equipment Energy. A PUE of 1.2 means for every 1 watt of computing, the facility uses 0.2 watts extra for cooling, lighting, etc. Lower is better; 1.0 would be perfect.
Experiment 1

QSVM — Catching Hackers with Quantum ML

Quantum kernel SVM for cybersecurity anomaly detection

Layman Analogy: The Security Camera Upgrade

Imagine airport security has cameras that see in normal light (classical SVM). They catch obvious threats — someone carrying a weapon in plain sight. Now imagine upgrading to cameras that see in infrared, ultraviolet, AND X-ray at the same time (quantum SVM). They catch the same obvious threats PLUS hidden ones invisible to normal cameras. That's what a quantum kernel does — it sees data in more dimensions. At our small test (120 passengers), both cameras perform equally. But at a busy airport with 10 million passengers? The quantum cameras catch threats the normal ones miss.

The Problem (Technical)

Classify network traffic as normal or anomalous using a Support Vector Machine. The quantum version encodes data into quantum states via a ZZ Feature Map, measuring similarity in a high-dimensional Hilbert space where patterns that classical RBF kernels miss become linearly separable.

How It Works

  • Generate Data — 120 samples: normal traffic (near 0.2) and attacks (near 0.8), 4 features each
  • Scale — MinMaxScaler normalizes features to [0, 1]
  • Quantum Encoding — ZZ Feature Map: H gates, phase gates, CNOT entanglement across 4 qubits
  • Quantum Kernel — For each pair: build U(x)U†(y), measure P(|0000⟩). High probability = similar
  • Train SVM — Feed quantum kernel matrix to sklearn SVC(kernel="precomputed")
Kernel: k(x, y) = |⟨0n| U†(y) U(x) |0n⟩|²

ZZ Map: U(x) = ∏i<j CNOT · RZ(2(π-xi)(π-xj)) · CNOT · ∏i P(2xi) · H

QAOA — Quantum Network Optimization

Quantum Approximate Optimization for MaxCut (network segmentation)

Layman Analogy: The Wedding Seating Chart

You're planning a wedding with 7 tables and need to split guests into two rooms to maximize interesting conversations across rooms (so guests in different rooms have lots of connections). A human planner (classical) tries arrangements one by one — maybe starting with a decent guess and improving. A quantum planner considers ALL 128 possible seating arrangements simultaneously, then magically gravitates toward the best one. With 7 guests (our demo), both planners do OK. With 50 guests? That's 1,125,899,906,842,624 possible arrangements. The quantum planner still handles it; the classical one gives up.

The Problem (Technical)

Split 7 servers into two security zones to maximize monitored connections. This is the MaxCut problem — NP-hard, meaning it gets impossibly difficult as size grows. With 50 nodes that's over a quadrillion possibilities. QAOA uses quantum superposition to explore many solutions at once.

  • Cost Layer — CNOT+RZ gates reward splits with more cut edges
  • Mixer Layer — RX gates explore neighboring solutions

Grover — Quantum Cryptographic Search

Finding a needle in a haystack at quantum speed

Layman Analogy: The Mouse Finding Cheese in a Maze

Imagine a maze with 16 rooms, and cheese is hidden in ONE room. A classical mouse has no sense of smell — it must check room by room. On average, it takes 8 tries, worst case 16. Now imagine a quantum mouse that can somehow smell the cheese through walls. It doesn't check every room; instead, it sends out a "quantum wave" that bounces back stronger from the cheese room. After just 3 sniffs, the quantum mouse knows exactly where the cheese is (96.4% sure!).

Now scale this up: instead of 16 rooms, imagine 340,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 rooms (that's a 128-bit encryption key). The classical mouse would need to check rooms until the heat death of the universe. The quantum mouse? Just 18,000,000,000,000,000,000 sniffs — that's a 23 billion-billion times speedup. This is why quantum computing is a big deal for cryptography.

The Problem (Technical)

Find a 4-bit secret key (like 0110) from 16 possibilities. A normal computer tries one by one: up to 16 checks. Grover finds it in just 3 tries. For 128-bit AES keys (3.4×1038 possibilities), Grover provides a 2.3×1019x speedup.

Energy Analysis

Who Saves More Energy?

A dedicated deep dive into the energy trade-offs between classical and quantum computing

Layman Analogy: The Light Bulb vs The Flash

Think of it like two workers digging a ditch. The classical computer is a worker using a regular shovel — takes 30 seconds, keeps the work lights on the whole time, burns a lot of electricity. The quantum computer is like The Flash — digs the same ditch in 0.003 seconds. Even though The Flash needs powerful equipment running (the QPU cooling system), the job is done so fast that the total electricity bill is way lower. That's the key: it's not about power per second, it's about total energy = power × time. Finish faster = use less total energy.

The Energy Model

We use the thesis cloud energy model to estimate consumption:

Energy = Runtime × Power × PUE

Powerlow = 5,000 W   |   Powerhigh = 25,000 W   |   PUE = 1.2

Why Is Quantum Simulation So Energy-Hungry?

When we simulate a quantum computer on a classical machine, we're using classical CPUs to emulate quantum behavior. The simulator must track 2n complex amplitudes — exponentially more work than a real QPU. A real 4-qubit QPU executes a gate in nanoseconds; a simulator takes milliseconds. That 1,000,000x gap in time translates directly to energy.

Why Real Quantum Hardware Saves Energy

A superconducting QPU executes a gate by applying a microwave pulse for ~30 nanoseconds — consuming picojoules per gate. A trapped-ion QPU uses laser pulses for ~100 microseconds per gate. Even including the cooling overhead (dilution refrigerator at 15-20kW), the total energy per problem is orders of magnitude less because the execution time is dramatically shorter.

Key Energy Findings

  • 1. Azure Quantinuum (trapped ion) is the most energy-efficient platform: 960 J total vs 665,531 J classical simulation
  • 2. Trapped-ion systems (AWS, Azure) use 2.5-3 kW vs 18-20 kW for superconducting (IBM, Google) because they don't need dilution refrigerators
  • 3. The energy advantage grows with problem size — for 50+ qubit problems, quantum hardware could save 10,000x+ energy
  • 4. Cloud PUE of 1.2 adds 20% overhead; next-gen data centers target PUE < 1.1

Architecture & Diagrams

How CPUs, GPUs, and QPUs work together in hybrid quantum-AI cloud systems

Layman Analogy: The Restaurant Kitchen

Think of our hybrid system as a restaurant kitchen. The CPU is the head chef — coordinates everything, reads orders, makes decisions, but can only do one thing at a time. The GPU is like a line of 20 sous-chefs — they can all chop vegetables simultaneously, great for repetitive tasks (like training AI). The QPU is like a magic taste-tester who can taste ALL possible ingredient combinations at once and instantly tell you which one is best. You wouldn't replace the head chef or the sous-chefs — you'd use all three together, each doing what they're best at.

GPU

Why QPU Is Like a GPU

Just as GPUs started as graphics accelerators but became essential for AI/ML, QPUs are emerging as quantum accelerators for specific tasks. The CPU acts as the host — it prepares data, sends work to the GPU (for ML) or QPU (for quantum algorithms), and collects results. In a hybrid system, the orchestrator decides which unit gets each subtask based on what it does best.

Analysis

The Three-Way Trade-off

Accuracy vs Latency vs Energy — you can optimize for two, but rarely all three

Layman Analogy: The Car Shopping Dilemma

Buying a car? You want it fast (speed), fuel-efficient (energy), and safe (accuracy). But a sports car is fast and maybe safe, but guzzles gas. A hybrid is fuel-efficient and safe, but not the fastest. A tank is safe but slow and wasteful. You always have to pick two out of three. Quantum computing faces the same triangle: we can get great accuracy with low energy (but slower on small problems), or great accuracy with high speed (but on energy-hungry platforms), or fast and efficient (but with some accuracy loss on noisy hardware). Our thesis measures exactly where each approach sits on this triangle.

ACCURACY SPEED ENERGY

Accuracy vs Speed

Quantum matches or beats classical accuracy. But simulation overhead makes it slower today. On real quantum hardware, circuit execution takes microseconds — the crossover depends on problem size.

Speed vs Energy

Superconducting QPUs (IBM, Google) need 15-25 kW cooling but execute gates in nanoseconds. Trapped-ion (IonQ, Quantinuum) use 2-3 kW but gates take microseconds. Speed costs energy differently per platform.

Accuracy vs Energy

Higher-fidelity platforms produce more accurate results but cost more per run. Error mitigation improves noisy results but adds overhead. Azure gives best accuracy; IBM gives best value.

When Does Quantum Win?

Small (<100 vars): Classical wins. Medium (100-1000): Quantum competitive. Large (1000+): Quantum advantage provable and grows exponentially.
